GIGW 3.0 Statement 5.2.9: Sensory Characteristics (WCAG 1.3.3)

Analysis and Requirements

**GIGW Statement**Instructions provided for understanding and operating content do not rely solely on sensory characteristics of components such as shape, colour, size, visual location, orientation, or sound.
**WCAG Reference****Success Criterion 1.3.3 Sensory Characteristics**
**WCAG Level****Level A**
**Clause in Short Form**Instructions Must Not Rely on Sensory Cues Alone
**Goal**To ensure content is accessible to users who cannot perceive shape, size, location, or orientation, such as the visually challenged.
**Requirement**Content and instructions that rely on sensory characteristics must **also** provide textual identification or programmatic information.

Success Examples ✅

Success Example 1: Relying on Shape and Textual Label

Scenario: Instructions tell the user to click a specific button based on its shape and text.

To proceed to the next step, click the **round button labelled "GO"**.

**Compliance Justification:** The instruction does not rely solely on the sensory characteristic (shape, "round"). It provides the necessary **textual identifier ("GO")**, making the instruction accessible to a screen reader user who can't see the shape.

Success Example 2: Relying on Location and Textual Identification

Scenario: Instructions guide the user to a button based on its general position and a clear label.

To finalize your application, click the button labelled **"SUBMIT"** in the lower right corner.

**Compliance Justification:** Although it mentions location ("lower right corner"), the primary means of identifying the target is the clear and unique **textual label ("SUBMIT FINAL")**. The location information is supplementary.

Failure Examples ❌

Failure Example 1: Relying on Shape and Color Alone

Scenario: Instructions guide the user based on shape, with no textual label.

Click the **round blue button** for immediate assistance and the **square red button** for general queries.

**Compliance Justification:** The screen reader would only announce "button" for both. The user cannot distinguish between the **"round blue"** and **"square red"** options, as the instructions rely solely on shape and color to convey the functional difference.

Failure Example 2: Relying on Auditory Cues Alone

Scenario: Instructions prompt the user based on a specific sound.

The system is ready to accept your voice command when you hear the **high-pitched chime**.

**Compliance Justification:** This fails for users who are **deaf or hard of hearing** as they cannot perceive the **high-pitched chime** sound cue. There is no alternative textual or visual indication that the system is ready (e.g., "Ready" text on screen).

Summary of Conformity

WCAG Reference WCAG Level Key Action for Compliance
SC 1.3.3 Sensory Characteristics A Ensure all instructions and content identification provide a **textual or programmatic alternative** to sensory cues (shape, size, color, location, sound). Use labels, text, and icons in addition to visual or auditory design.
