GIGW 3.0 Statement 5.2.32: Headings and Labels (WCAG 2.4.6)

Analysis and Requirements

**WCAG Reference****Success Criterion 2.4.6 Headings and Labels**
**WCAG Level****AA**
**Clause in Short Form**Descriptive Headings and Labels
**Goal**To help users quickly orient themselves within the page content and understand the purpose of every form control.
**Requirement**Headings must clearly state the topic of the section below them. Labels must clearly state the purpose of the input field they are associated with.

Example for Success Criteria ✅

Success Example 1: Descriptive Headings (Topic)

Scenario: A page with sections detailing different licensing requirements.

Section: Vehicle Licensing Requirements

This section outlines the documents needed for different vehicles.

Sub-section: Light Motor Vehicles (LMV)

Details on LMV registration and inspection...

Sub-section: Heavy Transport Vehicles (HTV)

Details on HTV weight restrictions and permits...

**Compliance Justification:** The headings clearly and concisely describe the **topic** of the content that follows. A screen reader user can navigate directly to the "Heavy Transport Vehicles (HTV)" section knowing exactly what information it contains. This meets the SC 2.4.6 requirement.

Success Example 2: Descriptive Labels (Purpose)

Scenario: A standard government online application form.

**Compliance Justification:** The labels **"Full Legal Name"** and **"Gender Identification"** clearly describe the **purpose** of the respective fields. All inputs, including radio buttons, are correctly associated with explicit, descriptive labels, ensuring screen reader users and those with cognitive disabilities can confidently enter the required data.

Example for Failure Criteria ❌

Failure Example 1: Generic or Redundant Headings

Scenario: A page uses the same heading for multiple distinct articles.

News Article

Summary of new infrastructure project...

News Article

Summary of new education policy...

**Compliance Justification:** The heading **"News Article"** is generic and repeated. A screen reader user navigating by headings will hear "News Article, News Article..." without knowing which section refers to which topic. The heading fails to describe the **topic or purpose** of the content below it, failing SC 2.4.6.

Failure Example 2: Labels that are Placeholders (Hidden Labels)

Scenario: A form where the developer relies on the placeholder attribute instead of a visible, associated label.

**Compliance Justification:** While the placeholder text gives a visual cue, it often disappears when the user starts typing and is not considered a programmatically determined label for accessibility purposes (unless hidden labels like `aria-label` or `aria-labelledby` are correctly used, but even then, visual labels are AA best practice). The lack of a clear, persistent, and programmatically associated label fails SC 2.4.6.
